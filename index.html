Introduction

It is often assumed that crime increases on weekends due to nightlife and increased social activity. In this analysis, we investigate whether crime in San Francisco actually becomes more frequent on weekends and, if not, whether its patterns change in more subtle ways.

Using SF crime data, we compare weekdays and weekends across three crime types: assault, burglary, and theft. Our main finding is that the weekend effect is not primarily about higher crime volume, but about a shift in when crime occurs.

Nighttime share by crime type

The most visible change appears for assault. Compared with weekdays, a noticeably larger share of assaults happens at night on weekends. Burglary and theft also shift slightly toward the night, but much less dramatically.

This suggests that weekend dynamics affect violent crime more strongly than property crime, likely reflecting changes in nightlife and late-evening social activity.

Spatial context of SF crime patterns

The map complements the temporal analysis by showing that crime is not evenly distributed across the city. Some neighborhoods contain much denser concentrations of incidents than others, which suggests that place matters alongside time.

Although our main story focuses on weekdays versus weekends, the spatial distribution helps contextualize how urban structure and activity centers may shape when and where crime is reported.

Hourly weekend profile by crime type

This interactive chart makes it easier to inspect the 24-hour rhythm of each crime type separately. Assault becomes more concentrated late at night, while theft follows a more routine daily pattern and burglary shows a smaller shift.

Together with Figure 1, this supports the main narrative of the page, weekends do not simply generate more crime, but instead reorganize crime in time, especially for violent incidents.

Conclusion

Crime does not appear to increase dramatically on weekends in total volume. Instead, the main weekend effect is a shift in timing, with a stronger concentration of incidents during nighttime hours, most clearly for assault.

This distinction matters because aggregate totals can hide meaningful behavioral patterns. Looking only at the number of incidents would miss the fact that weekend crime changes in rhythm rather than scale.

Limitations and critical interpretation

  • Crime data reflects reported or recorded incidents, not all incidents that occur.
  • Observed differences may partly reflect reporting practices or enforcement intensity, not only true behavioral change.
  • This analysis is descriptive and does not establish causality.
